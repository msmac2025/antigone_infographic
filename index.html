Teaching Antigone

A Visual Guide to Planning with Backward Design

Stage 1: Identify Desired Results

This initial stage is the foundation, focusing on the end goal. Before planning any activities, we must define what students should know, understand, and be able to do after studying "Antigone."

Enduring Understandings

Big ideas about moral conflict, civic duty, and tragedy that stick with students.

Essential Questions

Thought-provoking inquiries that drive discussion, like "When is it right to defy authority?".

Knowledge

Specifics students will know, from literary terms like 'hamartia' to the plot of the play.

Skills

Abilities students will master, such as citing evidence and constructing an argument.

Stage 2: Determine Acceptable Evidence

How will we know if students have achieved the desired results? This stage focuses on creating quality assessments *before* planning lessons, ensuring our teaching is targeted.

Summative Assessment

The culminating task is a multi-paragraph analytical essay where students argue a stance on a key theme, using textual evidence. This single task demonstrates mastery of the unit's core skills and knowledge.

Formative Assessments

These are ongoing checks for understanding that inform instruction. They range from formal quizzes to informal discussions, providing a complete picture of student progress.

Stage 3: Plan Learning Experiences

With goals and assessments set, we now design the path. This stage maps out the day-to-day lessons and activities that will equip students for success.

The Learning Sequence

Introduction (1-2 Days)

Hook students with the core concepts of Greek Tragedy and the unit's essential questions.

Reading & Analysis (5-7 Days)

Engage with the text through guided reading, character analysis, and formative quizzes.

Deeper Thematic Analysis (3-4 Days)

Facilitate Socratic seminars and close readings to explore complex themes like law vs. morality.

Essay Prep & Writing (3-4 Days)

Provide direct instruction on writing, followed by workshops, drafting, and peer review.

Assessment & Reflection (2-3 Days)

Students complete the summative essay and reflect on connections to modern society.

Further Exploration: Defiance in History

The themes of individual conscience versus state law in *Antigone* resonate throughout history. Explore these individuals who defied authority during the Civil Rights Movement, connecting their actions to the play's enduring questions.

Rosa Parks

Her refusal to give up her bus seat ignited the Montgomery Bus Boycott, a pivotal act of nonviolent resistance against segregation laws.

Research: Montgomery Bus Boycott, civil disobedience.

Martin Luther King Jr.

A leader of the nonviolent Civil Rights Movement, he advocated for defying unjust laws through peaceful protest and civil disobedience, as articulated in his "Letter from Birmingham Jail."

Research: "Letter from Birmingham Jail", nonviolent resistance.

Ruby Bridges

At six years old, she bravely integrated an all-white elementary school in New Orleans, facing hostility and demonstrating immense courage in challenging segregation.

Research: School desegregation, courage in the face of adversity.

The Little Rock Nine

A group of nine African American students who enrolled in Little Rock Central High School in 1957, challenging segregation and requiring federal troops for protection.

Research: Little Rock Crisis, federal intervention in civil rights.

John Lewis

A prominent leader of the Civil Rights Movement, known for his activism in the Freedom Rides and his leadership in the Selma to Montgomery marches, enduring violence for voting rights.

Research: Freedom Rides, Bloody Sunday, voting rights.
